The “Blue-Backed Speller” was made by Webster as a way to teach the new vocabulary and way of speaking developed by colonists (an Americanized type of English), instead of the British way (British English) of speaking and terms they were so used to pre Revolution This place was the birthplace of said founding father and where he spent most of his childhood growing up. There poets portray their thoughts, feelings and beliefs in using the very language Webster has helped developed.

John Paul Jones was a Revolutionary War hero. Originally born in Scotland, John Paul Jones eventually made way to America where he joined the Continental Army during the outbreak of the Revolutionary War. It was the year 1779 when he was placed in charge the ship the Bonhomme Richard along with 4 small ships. It was while he and his crew were sailing around the British Isles when he and his crew were met by 2 much more powerful British ships, the Serapis and Countess of Scarborough. Despite being out matched, when asked by the British to surrender he shouted “I have not yet begun to fight!” Against the odds he managed to capture both ships making him a navy hero, although lost the Bonhomme Richard during the fight. It was at this house said naval hero had stayed in 1777.

Plimoth Plantation is a living history museum that shows how life was like in the Plymouth Colony centuries ago. Plymouth Colony was a colony made by a group of people known as Pilgrims. Pilgrims were people who were seeking religious freedom from the Church of England, thus set sail for America on the Mayflower. What makes the Plimoth Plantation unique is it not only shows how life was like from the perspective of just the Pilgrims, but also from the Native American’s perspective too, whom they had several conflicts with while trying to settle there. When coming to Massachusetts one should definitely stop by Plimoth Plantation because it’s not often you get to experience both sides of history.

It was the year 1770 when Paul Revere moved in with his family consisting of his wife Sarah, mother Deborah, and his 5 children. Together Revere and his family would stay until the year 1800. It was during this time that Paul Revere would join 50 other Patriots in protest of British taxation of the colonist without American representation in British Parliament by dumping British tea in Boston Harbor. This event would later be known as the Boston Tea Party. Though he participated in such a famous event, Paul Revere is even more well known for his famous ride on April 16, 1775 during which he rode with William Dawes to warn Patriot leaders Samuel Adams and John Hancock hiding in Lexington, Massachussetts, of the plans of the British army to to capture them and their weapon supply hidden in Concord, Massachusetts. Why not come to Massachusetts to view the home of a Patriot war hero who made such a great contribution that lead the United States to it's victory in the Revolutionary War.

Towering at 306 feet and 4.5 inches tall and composed of magnesian limestone, stands The Bennington Battle Monument. The monument was completed in the year 1891 in honor of the Battle of Bennington. The Battle of Bennington occurred on August 16th 1777 during the Revolutionary War when British General John Burgoyne attacked Bennington, Vermont in order to restock on supplies after running low on them after a siege on American forts along Lake Champlain-Hudson River. Against a joint siege by British Army and its allies: Canadians, Indians, and Loyalist; the Patriots managed to pull a victory. This win would later prove significant in its impact when the win in Bennington helped the Patriots win the Battle of Saratoga and win the Revolutionary War.
